6.1 Einsteinâ€™s Static Universe

Shortly after completing the general theory of relativity, Einstein went on to apply his new theory to the universe as a whole !! The structure of the universe beyond our Milky Way galaxy was then completely unknown, thus Einstein made some assumptions.

Einstein assumed that the universe is isotropic on average. (It looks more or less the same in all directions). We now know that if the distribution of galaxies is smoothed over distances of 300 million light years or so, it does appear to be homogeneous and isotropic.

The universe cannot be homogeneous and isotropic unless space itself has these properties and thus, the curvature of space should be (on average) the same at all places and in all directions. As discussed earlier there are only 3 types of such spaces: a flat Euclidean space, a closed spherical space, and an open hyperbolic space.

Einstein also assumed that the average characteristics of the universe, such as the average density of stars, do not change with time.

But as it turns out the equations of General Relativity have no solutions with these properties. The problem is that masses distributed in the universe are pulled together by gravity and refuse to stay at rest. The theory was suggesting that the universe couldnâ€™t be static.

Einstein said that the equations for General Relativity had to be modified to endow vacuum. As per these modified equations the energy density \(\rho_{\nu}\) is constant everywhere (Einstein called it the cosomological constant). The vacuum pressure \(P_{\nu}\) is related to the vacuum every density as:

\[ P_{\nu} = \rho_{\nu} \]

Negative pressure is commonly referred to as tension. So if the vacuum has tension why doesnâ€™t it suck everything in ? The Reason being in order to produce a force, we need a difference in pressure. The vacuum pressure is same everywhere and thus we should not expect any shrinkage (or expansion).

The force of gravity in General Relativity depends both on the energy( or mass ) density. It is proportional to:

\[ \rho + 3P \]

For ordinary matter, pressure is negligible, so we are used to thinking about gravity as being dependent only on mass. However, for the vacuum, the pressure has the same magnitude as the energy density. Thus, we find that the gravitational force of the vacuum is proportional to:

\[ \rho_{\nu} + 3P_{\nu} = -2 \rho_{\nu} \] Einstein realized that by adding a cosmological constant to his equations, he could balance the gravitational attraction of matter with the gravitational repulsion of the vacuum. All he needed was a matter density with \(\rho_m = 2 \rho_{\nu}\), to balance the gravitational effect of vacuum. He thereby obtained a solution that describes a static universe. This solution has a closed, spherical geometry, with the radius determined by the matter density. For the density given by recent measurements, the corresponding circumference is about 100 billion light years.
