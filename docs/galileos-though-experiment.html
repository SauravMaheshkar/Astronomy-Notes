5.1 Galileo‚Äôs though Experiment

In his work Dialogue, Galileo argued that the motion of objects under the action of gravity is independent of their mass, size, or any other intrinsic properties, as long as air resistance and other non-gravitational forces can be neglected. This was contradictory to the accepted Aristotelian viewpoint at the time (Heavier objects fall faster).

We know that Galileo experimented with marbles rolling down inclined planes and found that the motion was independent of the mass. He offered a proof on how Aristotle‚Äôs theory couldn‚Äôt be right:

Suppose that indeed a heavy rock falls faster than a light rock. Now, Imagine that they are tied together with a very light string. The slower-moving light rock‚Äôs motion should make the motion of the heavier rock somewhat slower. But if we view both the rocks as a system, it‚Äôs combined mass is more than the heavy rock and should therefore fall faster.

This contradiction demonstrated that Aristotle‚Äôs theory was inconsistent.

NOTE: In the absence of forces, an object moves at a constant velocity along a straight line in spacetime, regardless of what the object is made of. It‚Äôs as if the object in space and time is a property of spacetime itself.

Let‚Äôs take this analogy a bit further. Suppose that instead of a cabin, we lock ourselves in a falling elevator. All objects in the elevator and the elevator itself, will fall at the same rate. We will not be able to feel our weight because the floor itself is falling at the same rate. If we drop an object it will float next to us. The same kind of weightlessness is experienced in spaceships.

The motion of objects in gravitational fields looks very similar to inertial motion.

But, there‚Äôs a important difference. Gravity makes objects accelerate towards the Earth‚Äôs center, thus their wordlines are no longer straight.

This led Einstein to conclude that in the presence of gravity, objects still move along the straightest lines in spacetime, but spacetime itself is curved. The idea is that massive bodies curve spacetime around them.

For example, the spacetime in the vicinity of the Sun gets curved. Thus, the Earth does not move along a straight line at a constant velocity (as it would in the absence of any gravitating bodies), but instead it moves around the Sun. The Earth‚Äôs worldline is in fact the straightest line in this curved spacetime. (Such lines are also called geodesics). Note that a geodesic line in spacetime does not necessarily correspond to the straightest trajectory in space. For example, the Earth‚Äôs elliptic orbit around the Sun is certainly not the straightest possible path.
